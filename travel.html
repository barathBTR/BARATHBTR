Istanbul: Bridging the gap between East and West, one ferry at a time

With a skyline featuring a mosque dome next to a cathedral, a glinting skyscraper shadowing an ancient Jewish hospital and a high-end shopping street ending in a traditional bazaar, Istanbul bridges the gap between Europe and Asia.

By Riya Baibhawi : “Merhaba! Welcome to Istanbul. Could you step back and look into the camera, please?” said the immigration official as I landed in Turkey on January 27. Who knew that just a week later, this beautiful country would witness its worst natural disaster in a century - an earthquake that would kill nearly 50,000 people? But as of now, I was in Constantinople (now known as Istanbul), a city that conjured the Romans, the Ottomans and still continues to bewitch people across all faiths and religions.

Istanbul or Islambol was established in May 1453 by Fatih Sultan Mehmet as the centre of Islam. The 21-year-old prince had annihilated the unbreakable walls of Constantinople, standing true to the Prophet’s words and bringing the great Byzantine Empire to its knees.

I was transfixed the moment I first soaked in the beauty of Istanbul. The squeaks of seagulls on the Bosphorus Strait, the aroma of spices and fresh fishes in the Kapalicarsi or Grand Bazaar, the kaleidoscope of colours in Karakoy, the transcontinental city that stood largely on the masterpieces of Mimar Sinan and Genoese merchants, could charm anybody.

The unique mix

As I spent my days in the financial capital of Turkiye, commuting between the serenity of Florya and the historic waterfront neighbourhood of Sultanahmet, I could hear Azan from the 1,486-year-old Hagia Sophia mosque located in the vicinity. However, as I walked east from the Eminonu neighbourhood, through its highly gentrified and cobblestoned streets, to the Golden Horn, I could see a part that one could easily mistake for Lisbon or Paris. This part of Istanbul encompassed a highly organised tram system, glinting nightlife and opulent shopping streets amongst other things, thanks to the Father of the Turkish Republic, Mustafa Kemal Ataturk.

A foodie’s paradise

Istanbul is a boon for those with a sweet tooth. As somebody who had already spent months in Egypt, I was familiar with most of the sweets the Mediterranean city had to offer. However, what distinguished Istanbul from others was its historic Ottoman traditions.

For instance, the Turks served Kahvesi (Turkish coffee) with water (Lokum/Turkish delight in ancient times). It was believed that if the guest picked water first, they were hungry and the host was obliged to serve them food. And, if they picked coffee first and drank water on top of that, then the guest was believed to dislike the taste of the coffee.
